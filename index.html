About

Uncovering the biophysics of ciliary signaling

The Ha Lab combines electrophysiology, biophysics, and cell biology to decode how primary cilia function as sensory organelles. Using ciliary patch-clamp recordings, live-cell imaging, and quantitative models, we map real-time ion channel dynamics to bridge the gap between ciliary signaling and systemic physiological function.

Our work spans from single-channel biophysics to whole-organism physiology, with applications in understanding ciliopathies.

We are committed to rigorous, reproducible science and to training the next generation of scientists while advancing exciting ciliary physiology at the University of Pittsburgh. Our lab fosters a collaborative environment where trainees harness specialized biophysical techniques to uncover the fundamental principle of primary cilia.

Kotdaji Clara Ha, Ph.D.

Principal Investigator

Dr. Ha leads the laboratory's research programs in cilia physiology and electrophysiology. Her work focuses on ion channel function in primary cilia, with an emphasis on how electrical signals are generated and propagated within these specialized compartments.

Research

What we study

Ciliary Ion Channels

We use patch-clamp electrophysiology to characterize ion channels in the ciliary membrane, examining their gating, selectivity, and regulation by signaling molecules.

Mechanosensation or chemosensation

Primary cilia are exquisite sensors. We investigate how fluid flow, deflection, mechanical, or chemical load activate ciliary signaling cascades at the molecular and cellular level.

Cilia Biophysics

We develop quantitative models of ciliary electrical signaling, integrating experimental data with computational approaches to understand ciliary dynamics.

Ciliopathy Disease Models

Defective cilia underlie a broad spectrum of human diseases. We use cellular and model organism systems to understand the electrophysiological basis of ciliopathies.

Signal Compartmentalization

The ciliary compartment maintains distinct ion concentrations such as calcium. We study how the diffusion barrier at the ciliary base controls signal fidelity and amplification.

Methods Development

We develop and refine tools for recording electrical activity directly from primary cilia.
